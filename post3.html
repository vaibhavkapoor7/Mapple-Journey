5 Common Mistakes When Arriving in Canada (and How to Avoid Them)

When someone arrives in Canada, it's not just another life experience, but also a huge and courageous change that person can make. Everything becomes new, including the language, the food, the culture, and the climate. That's why it's normal to make mistakes at first.

Here, I share the five most common mistakes among newcomers, and how to prevent them and make your experience much easier and more positive.

By Maple Journey Team • Updated Oct 2025 • 3 min read

  • Tips
  • Newcomer
  • Canada

1. Underestimating the cost of living

Source / Credit: Kanan.co — “Canada Living Expenses Distribution using Statistics model.”

Many people arrive without properly calculating their expenses and quickly run out of savings.

Many arrive with no idea of ​​how much they'll spend and are very confident.

Suddenly, they have no savings left, which is why we advise them to prepare a budget for at least six months, taking into account where they'll be living, transportation, and food. Use apps like Money Mgr. That helps you manage your finances much better.

2. Not improving English or French

Credit: Mennonite New Life Centre of Toronto (MNLCT).

Sticking to the basics greatly limits your job and social opportunities.

That's why we recommend enrolling in free courses (LINC or Clic). Practice every day and take advantage of the social activities that come your way.

3. Not adapting the resume to the Canadian format

Credit: Template design © Canva – Canadian Resume Example

Remember that this is a new country, and requirements are constantly changing.

That's why it's recommended to use the Canadian format. No photo, but with specific achievements and keywords related to the position.

You can search for free resume-building workshops.

4. Signing a lease without knowing the area

Credit: Map data © 2018 Google. Image captured from Google Maps.

It's highly recommended to research where you'll be living to avoid unsafe neighborhoods or places that are far from your work.

If you can, stay temporarily in an Airbnb or shared housing while you look in person.

5. Not building a credit history

Source: VantageScore 3.0 — © VantageScore Solutions, LLC.

In Canada, credit is essential for everything: renting, buying, or financing a car. Open a bank account and apply for a secured credit card as soon as possible. Always pay on time to build your score.
